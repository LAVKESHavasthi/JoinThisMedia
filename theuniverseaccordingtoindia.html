The Universe According to India

The concept of the universe in India has deep roots in ancient philosophy, astronomy, and cosmology. Indian cosmological models explore the nature of the universe, its creation, and the cycles of existence through both spiritual and scientific perspectives. The Indian view of the universe has been significantly shaped by Vedic, Upanishadic, and Puranic texts, and by great philosophers and sages who have elaborated on its vastness and its cyclical nature.

Vedic Cosmology

In the Vedic tradition, the universe is viewed as a manifestation of divine energy. The Vedas, the oldest sacred texts in India, contain various hymns and verses that provide glimpses into the ancient understanding of the cosmos. According to Vedic cosmology:

  • Cosmic Order (Rta): The universe operates in a cyclical manner governed by the divine law of cosmic order, called Rta. This concept suggests that the universe is bound by a constant flow of time and energy, and all living beings are connected to this order.
  • The Three Realms (Lokas): Ancient Indian texts describe the universe as divided into three realms: the Swarga (heavenly realms), the Prithvi (earthly realm), and the Patal (underworld). Each realm is inhabited by different beings and governed by distinct principles.

Hindu Cosmology and the Cyclic Universe

Hindu cosmology, especially as explained in texts like the Mahabharata and the Vishnu Purana, presents the universe as eternal and cyclical, undergoing a constant process of creation, preservation, and dissolution:

  • The Four Yugas: The universe is divided into four ages (Yugas) — Krita Yuga (Golden Age), Treta Yuga (Silver Age), Dvapara Yuga (Bronze Age), and Kali Yuga (Iron Age). Each Yuga has its own characteristics and its duration, and they repeat in a continuous cycle.
  • Creation and Dissolution: According to Hindu cosmology, creation occurs through the will of the supreme god, Brahma. Each universe has a lifespan during which it is created, sustained by Vishnu, and eventually dissolved by Shiva. This cosmic cycle is known as Pralaya, or the period of dissolution, before a new creation begins.

The Concept of Brahman

The universe, in the philosophical context, is also seen as a manifestation of Brahman, the supreme, unchanging reality. The Upanishads describe Brahman as both immanent and transcendent — it is the essence that permeates all of existence but also beyond it. The universe is believed to emanate from Brahman and ultimately returns to it in a cycle of birth, life, and dissolution.

The Scientific Perspective: Indian Contributions to Cosmology

India's contribution to the scientific study of the universe can be traced back to ancient times. Indian scholars were pioneers in the fields of astronomy and mathematics:

  • Aryabhata (476–550 CE): Aryabhata was one of the first scientists to describe the universe with a heliocentric model, explaining that the Earth rotates on its axis. He also calculated the value of pi and proposed an early version of the concept of gravity.
  • Varahamihira (505–587 CE): A leading astronomer and mathematician, Varahamihira's work Pancha Siddhantika describes the positions of celestial bodies, eclipses, and the motions of planets, influencing later astronomy.
  • Bhaskara I and II: The two Bhaskaras made significant contributions to the understanding of planetary motion, algebra, and trigonometry, advancing knowledge of the cosmos in their respective times.

The Universe and the Concept of Time

In Indian cosmology, time is considered cyclical and infinite. The concept of Kalachakra (wheel of time) is central to Indian philosophy, signifying the endless cycles of creation and destruction. Time is not linear but flows in recurring cycles of ages, with each cycle representing a period of evolution, stagnation, and dissolution.

The Indian Influence on Global Cosmology

The ancient Indian concepts of the universe and time have influenced other cultures, including those in the Islamic Golden Age and the European Renaissance. Indian cosmological ideas were transmitted to the West through the translation of Sanskrit texts and through Islamic scholars, who made important contributions to astronomy based on Indian principles.

Cosmology and Spirituality

In India, the understanding of the universe is not just an intellectual pursuit but also a spiritual journey. The cosmos is viewed as a reflection of divine principles, and by contemplating the universe, one can gain a deeper understanding of oneself and the divine. Meditation, yoga, and other spiritual practices in India are often used to align oneself with the cosmic order and achieve spiritual enlightenment.
